# **Research Outline: The Dynamics of Viral-Based Policy in Indonesia**

### **1\. Research Background**

**The Phenomenon of "No Viral, No Justice"**

In contemporary Indonesia, a distinct governance pattern has emerged where public policy implementation and revision are increasingly driven by viral social media campaigns rather than traditional deliberative processes. Colloquially termed *"No Viral, No Justice"* (or *\#PercumaLaporPolisi* in law enforcement contexts), this phenomenon represents a shift from systematic, evidence-based policymaking to what scholars describe as **"Viral-Based Policy."** This concept refers to a reactive model where government attention and responsiveness are contingent upon the volume and intensity of digital outrage.

**The Failure of Formal Channels**

The proliferation of this phenomenon indicates a breakdown in formal democratic feedback loops. Traditional channels for public aspiration—such as local deliberation forums (*Musrenbang*), legislative representation, and official complaint handling systems—are increasingly viewed by the public as stagnant or unresponsive. As a result, Indonesian citizens have pivoted to "Connective Action," utilizing platforms like X (formerly Twitter), TikTok, and Instagram to bypass bureaucratic inertia.

**Case Studies of Reactive Governance**

Recent history provides compelling evidence of this shift. The 2024 *\#PeringatanDarurat* movement, which successfully pressured the legislature to halt revisions to the Regional Election Law, and the *\#IndonesiaButuhKerja* protests against the Omnibus Law, demonstrate how digital mobilization creates "policy shocks." Unlike traditional lobbying, these movements are decentralized, rapid, and emotionally charged, forcing state actors to engage in "fire-fighting" governance—prioritizing issues that threaten political stability via algorithmic popularity rather than strategic urgency.

**Theoretical Context**

This research situates the Indonesian experience within the broader crisis of digital democracy. While social media was initially hailed as a tool for democratization, in Indonesia, it has arguably evolved into a *de facto* "supreme court of public opinion," where justice and policy revision are dispensed based on virality metrics. This raises critical questions about the sustainability, equity, and quality of policies born from algorithmic pressure rather than rigorous analysis.

**2\. Research Gap**

**Limitations of Existing Studies**

Current literature on digital activism in Indonesia (e.g., Lim, 2017; Nugroho, 2020\) predominantly focuses on the **input side** of the policy spectrum: detailing how social movements organize, how hashtags trend, and the sociology of digital citizenship. Conversely, public policy literature in Indonesia often analyzes the **output side**—the content of laws and regulations—without adequately accounting for the digital volatility that precipitated them.

**The Theoretical Disconnect**

There is a significant scarcity of research that rigorously bridges these two domains using a synthesized theoretical framework. Specifically, there is a lack of scholarship that:

1. **Mechanistic Interaction:** Explains the precise causal mechanism of how "Connective Action" (the chaotic, leaderless digital pressure) triggers a "Punctuated Equilibrium" (the sudden state response) in the Indonesian context.  
2. **The "Threshold of Virality":** Quantifies or qualifies the "tipping point" required for a viral issue to transition from online noise to a mandatory policy agenda item.  
3. **Quality of Outcome:** Evaluates the *substantive quality* of viral-based policies. Existing studies often celebrate the "victory" of the viral campaign but fail to analyze whether the resulting hasty policy decisions lead to long-term solutions or merely temporary political appeasement.

**The Proposed Contribution**

This research fills this gap by moving beyond descriptive analysis of "what happened" to an explanatory model of "how and why it works." By integrating **Bennett & Segerberg’s Logic of Connective Action** with **Baumgartner & Jones’s Punctuated Equilibrium Theory**, this study aims to formalize "Viral-Based Policy" not just as a social trend, but as a distinct, predictable mechanism of modern Indonesian governance. It challenges the assumption that digital responsiveness equates to democratic accountability, positing instead that it may represent a vulnerability in the state's institutional capacity.
